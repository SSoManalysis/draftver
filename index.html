Sonic Landscapes in the Digital Realm

An Examination of Music Performance in McGill University and Diversity, Equity and Inclusion (DEI)


McGill University's Schulich School of Music (SSoM) has long been one of Canada’s leading institutions for nurturing generations of music performers, scholars and researchers. With over 15 student music ensembles – including orchestras, jazz ensembles, contemporary music ensemble, opera to choirs – performance and ensemble playing are central to the student experience. Amid this rich array of performance activities, the School remains committed to equity, diversity and inclusion (EDI) through enhancing the diversity of its curriculum, repertoire, and teaching modalities.

Following the launch of the Schulich School of Music Performance Archive (SSoMPA), information about the School’s concert and performance history has become openly accessible. Thus this project aims to use the open data to evaluate the School’s endeavor to diversify its concert/recital programming. It also seeks to explore how these efforts aligns with the broader EDI discussions within Canadian society. It is hoped that a glimpse into SSoM's performance past could offer insights that could inform future EDI program development. Additionally, it aims to demonstrate how digital humanities and data mining can support institutions in assessing their success in designing EDI music performance curricula.

Evolution of EDI discussion in Canadian news

To understand how the topic of EDI evolved, all Canadian historical and current news sources on ProQuest between Jan 1990 and August 2023 were data mined using TDM Studio. The keywords and phrases used in searches included “diversity equity and inclusion” and variations of these terms and their acronyms in different orders and combinations. The longitudinal view yielded showed that:

  • there was an increase of EDI discussion in 1997-1999, and
  • it grew exponentially starting 2018.

This observation was then compared against SSoM’s concert programming trend. It reveals that the inclusion of works by BIPOC (Black, Indigenous, People of Color) composers surged earlier than the general EDI discussion in the Canadian society.
