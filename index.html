An Examination of Music Performance in McGill University and Diversity, Equity and Inclusion (DEI)


McGill University's Schulich School of Music (SSoM) has been one of the major institutions in Canada that nurture generations of music performers, scholars and researchers. There are currently 15 student music ensembles in the School ranging from orchestras, jazz ensembles, contemporary music ensemble, opera to choirs, and ensemble playing forms an important part of the student experience. Amid this vast amount of performance activities, the School remains committed to equity, diversity and inclusion (EDI) and one of the goals as stated in its Diversity Action Plan is to enhance the diversity of curriculum, repertoire and teaching modalities.

The performance open data and concert programs in the Performance Archives (SSoMPA) [Collection 030 - Schulich School of Music Performance Archive] were analyzed with the aim to evaluate the diversification endeavor in concert/recital programming, and examine the relationship between the latter and the impact of EDI discussion in Canadian newspapers. It is hoped that a glimpse into SSoM's EDI efforts in music performance could provide an indicative direction for its consideration as it continues its commitment in EDI and to demonstrate how digital humanities and data mining could help institutions evaluate their EDI success in designing music performance curriculum.

For a long time...

Classical music has been dominated by composers such as Mozart, Beethoven, Brahms and Copland, i.e. all white (dead) men. As Purdon (2023) writes, "it is no secret that music, particularly classical music, has struggled with diversity, equity, inclusion, access, and belonging (DEIAB) issues throughout its history". While there have been initiatives in diversifying repertoire, many often "do not go more than a performative level of advocacy (Rend√≥n, 2023). Marcho (2020) further reveals that conductors and directors tend to program familiar music and professional orchestra administrators and music educators favor well-known male composers over female composers. Nonetheless, as conductors and teachers are the ones determining the repertoire for their ensembles, considerations such as the transmission of values and development of identity across all music cultures could set a good foundation for a culturally responsive pedagogy (Cumberledge & Williams, 2023). Other ways to create lasting change in support of EDI could be through education and outreach, by fostering an environment where young musicians from diverse backgrounds can thrive, collaborating with community organizations, and reaching audiences that have been historically underrepresented in classical music (Purdon, 2023).
